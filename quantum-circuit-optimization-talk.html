Step 1 · the foundation

A complete equational theory for circuits

Clement, Heurtel, Mansfield, Perdrix & Valiron proved that a finite set of local circuit identities is complete: any two circuits that implement the same unitary can be turned into one another using only these rewrite moves.

The flavours of moves

  • Cancellations: H H = I, CNOT CNOT = I (a, e).
  • Phase arithmetic: phases add and merge; P(0)=I (b, c, d, k).
  • Commutations: push a phase or an X through a CNOT (f, i, l).
  • CNOT-network identities: three CNOTs make a SWAP (h); the “bridge” reroutes a CNOT network (g, j).
  • Hadamard–CNOT relation turning a CNOT into a CZ-like gadget (m), plus the larger rotated identities (n, o).

Why this matters for us

Completeness means simplification is, in principle, just a search over sequences of these local moves. No move ever changes the unitary, so we can apply them freely and keep whatever circuit is shortest.

The catch: the space of move-sequences is astronomically large, and the shortest path often gets longer before it gets shorter. That is exactly the problem we hand to a learned policy.

Specializing the axioms

The QC₀ axioms are stated for continuous phases P(θ) and rotations Rₓ(θ). We specialize them to our discrete gates: a phase is now one of {T, S, Z, T†}, an Rₓ becomes a phase conjugated by H, and so on. Each abstract axiom turns into one or more concrete identities on the seven gates above — the building blocks of our rewrite library.

Example 1: commuting a phase through a control

QC₀ has a continuous identity of the form Rz(θ) · CX = CX · Rz(θ) when the phase is on the control wire. In v1 we only need the discrete values. Taking θ = π/4 gives the concrete rule T · CX = CX · T; taking θ = π/2 gives S · CX = CX · S; taking θ = π gives Z · CX = CX · Z.

Example 2: adding phases

QC₀ also has phase addition: Rz(φ₁) · Rz(φ₂) = Rz(φ₁+φ₂). Choosing φ₁ = φ₂ = π/2 gives the v1 rule S · S = Z. Choosing two π/4 phases gives T · T = S. These are just arithmetic in the discrete phase group.

Step 4 · learning

Teaching a model to drive the rewriting

Greedy “always shorten” gets stuck: to reach a short circuit you often must make it longer first. We want a policy with intuition for those productive detours.

The policy

A policy is a neural network that reads the current circuit and outputs a probability for every legal move (position, rule). We sample a move from it, apply it, and repeat — a rollout.

Transformer on the gate list

For v1 the circuit is linearized into a token sequence — each gate becomes a token carrying (gate-type, qubit-1, qubit-2). A small RoPE transformer reads the sequence and, at each position, scores the rules. Illegal moves are masked out before we sample, so the policy only ever considers rewrites that actually match.

The self-play buffer

We have no labelled “correct” simplifications, so the model teaches itself:

  1. Take a random circuit.
  2. Roll out the current policy several times (with randomness).
  3. Keep the rollout that reached the shortest circuit; trim it at its best step.
  4. Drop the (circuit, move) pairs from that best play into a replay buffer.
  5. Train the policy to imitate its own best play (behaviour cloning).
  6. Repeat for many generations — each generation’s policy is a bit stronger, so its best rollouts get better, so the next round of training data improves.

The buffer is coverage-aware: it avoids forgetting rare rules, so the model keeps a balanced repertoire. (A second loop, GRPO, instead rewards rollouts by how much — and how quickly — they shrink the circuit.)

“Sample several, take the best” is the recurring theme — both when generating training data and at inference time. A single greedy rollout is brittle; the best of a handful is much stronger.

Step 6 · the wall

Where v1 hits a wall: CCX·CCX = I

Two Toffolis in a row are the identity. Since CCX = H₃·CCZ·H₃, the inner Hadamards cancel and the crux is CCZ·CCZ = I — a 26-gate circuit that should vanish.

The hand-found “gold” proof

We found a simplification by hand (shape-biased A* search): 172 local moves that take CCZ·CCZ all the way to the empty circuit. Crucially it grows first — it climbs from 26 gates well above 30 before collapsing to 0. Every step is verified to preserve the unitary.

What the model does instead

The trained v1 policy cannot find it. Every opening move makes the circuit longer with no immediate payoff, and the path is 172 steps deep — far beyond what sampling stumbles onto. Its rollouts wander and never get below ~25 gates.

The flat gate-list buries the structure that makes this circuit special. Lots of degrees of freedom (commuting gates, reorderings, phase shuffles) are spent on nothing. That motivated v2.
Step 7 · a better representation

v2: a layered, structured circuit

Instead of a flat list, a v2 circuit alternates single-qubit layers and CZ layers:

wire₀ · CZ₀ · wire₁ · CZ₁ · … · CZ₄₋₁ · wire₄   (L+1 single-qubit layers, L CZ layers)

  • Each wire segment on a qubit is a run of Tk·H pieces: Tk₁ H Tk₂ H … with each k ∈ {0,1,2,3}. Interior layers always end in H; the final layer drops the trailing H.
  • Each CZ layer is a set of CZ edges. Because CZs commute, the order inside a layer is irrelevant.
  • Leftover Paulis (ZzXx per wire) and an output permutation are tracked at the very end.

The size metric is CZ count + H count; the diagonal T-powers (T, S, T†) are considered “free”.

A small v2 circuit

Dashed purple bands mark CZ layers. The two adjacent purple boxes below deliberately repeat the same CZ(1,2) in consecutive layers: v2 keeps repeated layers explicit until a rule such as CZ_CANCEL can prove they should annihilate.

The representation bakes in the easy facts (CZs commute, Paulis push to the end, phases are integers mod 4) so the model can spend its effort on the genuinely hard structural moves.
Step 8a · quotienting out phase noise

Canonicalization: export Paulis, then push them right

The v2 wire alphabet only stores T-powers modulo 4: {0,1,2,3}. Whenever an identity produces a T-power modulo 8, we split off the Pauli part first.

T-powers mod 8 become T-powers mod 4 + Pauli

Because T4 = Z, any Tk with k ∈ {0,…,7} can be written as:

Tk = Z⌊k/4⌋ Tk mod 4

So if a local move creates, say, T6, v2 stores the wire element as T2 = S and exports a Z Pauli.

Then push the Pauli through the rest of the circuit

Paulis propagate through Clifford pieces by deterministic commutation rules. For example:

  • Z H = H X and X H = H Z: Hadamard swaps Pauli axes.
  • Through CZ(a,b), an X on one endpoint picks up a Z on the other endpoint.
  • At the end we store only a compact per-wire pair (z,x), meaning ZzXx.

Other structural canonicalization

  • Sort each CZ layer. CZs commute, so a layer is a set.
  • Drop empty CZ layers, fusing the wire segments on either side.
  • Drop trivial final segments (0,) = T⁰ = I.
  • Forward-merge wire segments past CZ layers that do not touch the wire, so every interior non-empty segment sits immediately before a CZ layer that does touch that qubit.
Step 8b · local wire canonicalization

Single-wire moves make the v2 alphabet small

Once Paulis are exported, each wire segment is a sequence of T-powers in {0,1,2,3} separated by H gates. The remaining easy one-wire identities are handled locally.

INNER_ZERO

An interior 0 means H T0 H = H H = I. So the neighbouring phase powers merge:

[..., a, 0, b, ...]  →  [..., (a+b) mod 4, ...]

HS3_SPLIT

A 2 means an S in the middle of a wire sequence. The identity (H S)3 = I lets us rewrite a local H S H pattern into a shorter segment, possibly exporting a Pauli that is pushed to the end.

This is why a separate [a,2,2,b] → [a+b+2] rule is redundant: one HS3_SPLIT plus one INNER_ZERO reaches it.

After these reductions, each wire is essentially a canonical word of the form {0,1,2,3}(H {1,3})* H?: the stored integers are mod-4 phases, zeroes and twos only appear where they cannot immediately collapse, and any Pauli residue is outside the word at the end.
Step 9 · the move set

The v2 move set

After the one-wire simplifications, the remaining v2 moves are about moving, cancelling, or temporarily expanding CZ-layer structure.

CZ_CANCEL

Two copies of the same CZ with clean wires between them annihilate.

CZ_COMMUTE

Slide one CZ across an adjacent layer when its wires are clean.

CZ_SWAP

Expand a CZ into two CZs, three pairs of Hs, and an output swap.

CZ_H_CZ

The essential CZ · H · CZ identity; it changes CZ parity without changing final permutation parity.

CZ_CZ_3

The 3-qubit CNOT-bridge / 5CX reroute, written in CZ-layer form.

CZ_PAIR_WS_MOVE / CZ_PAIR_SINGLE_MOVE

Move a whole wire segment, or one TkH element, through a cancelling pair of CZ layers.

Several expanding moves were added on purpose so the rewrite graph is connected in both directions — otherwise a circuit could reach a dead end with no legal way back toward the identity.

Step 10 · a model that fits the data

A graph neural network for a graph-shaped circuit

A flat transformer can’t see the layered structure, and there is no natural left-to-right order among CZs in a layer or among qubits. So v2 uses a graph neural network over a graph built from the circuit.

What is a vertex?

  • WIRESEG — one node per non-empty single-qubit segment (a TkH run on one qubit, between two CZ layers). Its sequence of T-powers is summarized into an embedding.
  • CZQUBIT — one node per (CZ layer, qubit) that the layer actually touches.

What is an edge?

  • CZ edge — joins the two CZQUBIT endpoints of an actual CZ gate.
  • CZ layer-neighbour — joins CZQUBITs in the same layer that don’t share a CZ (a “we’re in the same layer” cue, used by the 3-qubit moves).
  • Sequence fwd / bwd — directed edges along each qubit’s timeline, linking the next and previous node on the same wire.

How it scores moves

  1. Embed every node by its type.
  2. Run several rounds of message passing, where the message depends on the edge type, so each node gathers context from its neighbours.
  3. For each legal move, gather the embeddings of the nodes it touches (its anchors) and pass them through a small per-move-type head to produce one score.

Because everything is structural, the network is naturally indifferent to qubit labels and CZ ordering — it sees the circuit the way the canonical form does.

Step 11 · the payoff

v2 cracks CCZ·CCZ = I

Same handcrafted instance that stumped v1. The v2 GNN, sampling at temperature 1, drives it all the way down to the empty circuit — here it succeeds in 8 of 24 attempts, and we keep the best.

Watch the size climb above the start and then collapse to 0 — the same “grow, then shrink” shape as the hand proof, but discovered by the policy itself. A near-miss is included to show why we still sample several and take the best. YAY.
