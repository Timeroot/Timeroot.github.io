Smashing Polynomials
into Smooth Pieces

A number is “smooth” if it’s built from small prime factors. Polynomials can be smooth too — and we recently found a clean way to make every cubic polynomial spit out arbitrarily-smooth values. Scroll to play with it.

① First: what’s a smooth number?

Every whole number breaks apart into primes — its atomic building blocks. We call a number smooth when all of those prime atoms are small.

For example 720 = 2·2·2·2·3·3·5. Its biggest prime is just 5, so 720 is “5-smooth.” Compare 718 = 2·359 — that 359 is a big, ugly prime, so 718 is about as un-smooth as it gets. Smooth numbers are the friendly, cooperative ones: they show up all over cryptography, fast multiplication, and number theory because they’re so easy to take apart.

② Polynomials have “primes,” too

Here’s the beautiful analogy at the heart of this story. Just as numbers factor into primes, polynomials factor into irreducible polynomials — pieces that can’t be broken down any further (using only whole-number / fraction coefficients).

Numbers

60 = 2·2·3·5

building block = a prime
“size” of a block = the prime itself

Polynomials

x⁴−1 = (x−1)(x+1)(x²+1)

building block = an irreducible factor
“size” of a block = its degree (highest power)

So the “largest prime factor” of a number becomes the largest degree among the irreducible factors of a polynomial. A polynomial is smooth when it breaks into many low-degree pieces, and rough when one giant irreducible chunk refuses to split.

A degree-24 polynomial that stays in one piece is the rough extreme. One that crumbles into twelve quadratics (degree-2 bits) is gloriously smooth.

③ Why splitting makes smooth numbers

Here’s the bridge between the two worlds. Take a stubborn cubic f(x) = x³ − x − 1 and just start feeding it whole numbers. Most outputs are rough — they hide a big, ugly prime:

Every so often you stumble on a smooth one (like f(2)=5), but they’re rare and you can’t steer toward them. So here’s the question: can we manufacture smooth values on purpose — an endless supply of them?

The trick: feed f a special input, x = g(m)

Suppose we can find a polynomial g so that the composition f(g(x)) factors into smaller polynomials with whole-number coefficients. For the magic choice g = 16x⁸+32x⁶+20x⁴+4x² it breaks into four degree-6 pieces:

This is an algebraic identity — true for every x. So substitute any whole number x = m and it instantly becomes a fact about integers: the giant number f(g(m)) equals the product of the four piece-values. It can’t help but factor.

Watch a value shatter

Pick an input m. The number f(g(m)) is enormous — but it splits into four near-equal chunks, each about the fourth root of the whole:

That’s the whole point. Each piece has degree 6 — exactly a quarter of the total degree 24 — so each piece-value is about (f(g(m)))1/4 in size. No prime factor can exceed the biggest chunk, so the value is forced smooth. Contrast a careless choice like g = x⁸: now f(g(x)) = x²⁴ − x⁸ − 1 stays in one irreducible lump, the algebra guarantees nothing, and the values come out almost prime:

The smoothness score is just the exponent in that fourth root. We call it θ (theta): every value f(g(m)) is guaranteed to break into pieces no bigger than about (value)θ. Smaller θ ⇒ smaller guaranteed pieces ⇒ smoother numbers. Let’s pin it down.

④ Scoring the split: the number θ

We just watched f(g(x)) crack into four degree-6 pieces. In general, g of degree 8 makes f(g(x)) degree 3 × 8 = 24, and we capture the quality of the split with a single ratio:

smoothness score
θ = biggest piece’s degree total degree

θ = 1 means “one giant lump, no luck.”   Smaller θ = smoother.   The smallest θ we can force is the real prize.

Roots that share a color are “entangled” — they live in the same irreducible factor and can never be separated by rational algebra. Fewer, bigger color-families = rougher. Four equal families of six = our smooth sweet spot.

⑥ What we found: θ = ¼ for every cubic

Here’s the punchline. For any cubic polynomial you hand us, there is a clever degree-8 polynomial g with ordinary fraction coefficients so that f(g(x)) splits into four equal degree-6 pieces — pattern [6, 6, 6, 6], giving the record score

θ = 6 ⁄ 24 = ¼.

No single piece is ever bigger than a quarter of the whole. We verified this for cubics of both “personality types” (more on that below), and a search across dozens of random cubics hit 100% success. A peek at the trick. The secret ingredient is a little algebraic identity: if α is a root of a cubic x³ − A·x − B, then B·α + A²/4 is always a perfect square in the number system built from α (it equals (α² − A/2)²). That guaranteed square root is exactly the lever that lets us pry f(g(x)) apart into four matching pieces. Earlier searches kept “proving” it was impossible — but they were only looking at small whole-number coefficients and missed the fractions where the real witnesses live!

⑦ Two flavors of cubic: a Galois coda

Why do some cubics behave differently from others? The deepest answer is a 200-year-old idea from a duel-bound French teenager named Évariste Galois: attach to each polynomial a symmetry group describing how its roots can be shuffled.

A cubic has three roots. The Galois group records which scramblings of those three roots are “allowed” by algebra. For cubics there are only two possibilities:

S₃ — fully scrambled

All 6 rearrangements of the three roots are allowed. The roots are maximally tangled; knowing one tells you nothing about the others. This is the “generic” cubic.

x³−x−1,   x³−2,   x³−4x−1

C₃ — orderly rotation

Only 3 rearrangements survive — the roots cycle around like numbers on a clock. Pin down one root and the other two are determined. Rare and special.

x³−3x−1,   x³+x²−2x−1

There’s a wonderfully concrete test. Compute the discriminant of the cubic x³ + bx² + cx + d. If that number is a perfect square, the cubic is the orderly C₃ type; otherwise it’s the scrambled S₃. Try it:

Both of our smooth-cubic witnesses cover S₃ and C₃ — the trick doesn’t care which personality the cubic has, which is part of why we believe it works for absolutely every cubic.

⑧ Where it goes next

Two questions are still open and genuinely unsolved:

🧩 Can we beat ¼? Is there a construction that pushes the score below θ = ¼ for cubics — or is a quarter a hard wall? (For richer setups, scores can be made arbitrarily small, so the cubic story may not be over.)

🧩 Is it truly every cubic? We have a recipe plus overwhelming experimental evidence (every cubic we tried worked), but turning “we always find one” into a watertight proof — showing a certain geometric object always has a solution — is the remaining mathematical mountain.
