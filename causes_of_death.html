Causal Pathways of Human Death — a layered flow chart

What we colloquially call cause of death sits at many different levels of indirection. This chart sorts them top → bottom from upstream etiologies (smoking, infection, trauma, aging) through diseases / clinical syndromes, then acute organ-system failures, then cellular insult to the brain, then the final neurological mechanism, ending at death. Edges are tagged typical / common / possible / rare with a short mechanism note. Click any node or edge to read commentary in the panel on the right.

L4 ("disease") nodes loosely correspond to the kind of underlying cause coded on death certificates (ICD-10 chapter shown when applicable). The graph is a DAG, not a strict tree — most upstream nodes have several plausible descents, and most acute failures have several plausible upstream causes.

How to read it

A path from any top node to Death is one possible mechanistic chain. Most real deaths are the simultaneous traversal of several such chains; e.g. an MI causes both an arrhythmia (one chain) and pump failure (another), and either may be the one that actually finishes you. The chart is intentionally permissive: the existence of an edge means that pathway is documented, not that it dominates statistically.

Layers

  • L5 etiology — the upstream insult (a chronic risk like smoking; an infection; a piece of trauma).
  • L4 disease/syndrome — the named clinical condition that gets coded as the underlying cause of death.
  • L3 acute physiological failure — what is actually killing you in the last minutes/hours: arrest, shock, asphyxia, herniation, lethal electrolyte derangement.
  • L2 cellular insult — what those acute failures do to brain tissue: deprive it of O₂, of fuel, poison it, damage it physically, or inflame it.
  • L1 final neurological mechanism — the brain‑level mode of failure: most commonly mass neuronal death; rarely transmission blockade with intact neurons; or gross structural disruption.
  • L0 death — defined clinically as either irreversible cardiopulmonary cessation (which causes brain cessation within minutes) or irreversible whole‑brain function loss.
